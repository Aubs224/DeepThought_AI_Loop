**1. Executive Synthesis**

Direct air capture (DAC) technologies are a promising but nascent tool for removing CO₂ directly from the atmosphere. Achieving cost parity with fossil fuels and scaling to 1 gigaton (Gt) of CO₂ removal annually by 2035 is a formidable challenge. Current costs are high ($400–$1,000/tCO₂), and global installed DAC capacity is orders of magnitude below gigaton scale. While aggressive policy, technological breakthroughs, and sustained investment could drive rapid progress, substantial uncertainties remain regarding cost trajectories, supply chain bottlenecks, and market demand. The most plausible scenario is that DAC costs will fall and deployment will grow, but not fast enough to reach cost parity with fossil fuels or gigaton-scale removal by 2035 without major, coordinated interventions.

---

**2. Why This Question Is Hard**

- **Technological Maturity:** DAC is early-stage; few plants exist, and most are pilot-scale.
- **Cost Structure:** High energy requirements and material costs dominate OPEX and CAPEX.
- **Market Dynamics:** There is not yet a robust, reliable market for CO₂ removal at scale.
- **Policy Uncertainty:** Government support is volatile and fragmented.
- **Scale of Challenge:** 1 Gt CO₂/year is ~1,000× current DAC capacity; fossil fuel costs are low and subsidized.
- **Comparative Benchmarks:** Competing negative emissions (e.g., reforestation, BECCS) may be cheaper or more scalable in the near term.

---

**3. Current Forces and Constraints**

- **Forces Accelerating DAC:**
    - Increasing climate ambition (net-zero pledges, corporate offsets)
    - Growing investment (e.g., Frontier, Breakthrough Energy)
    - Early government subsidies (US Inflation Reduction Act, EU Innovation Fund)
    - Learning-by-doing and modularity potential

- **Constraints:**
    - High current costs ($400–$1,000/tCO₂)
    - Limited supply chains for sorbents, equipment, and renewable energy
    - Siting and infrastructure (land, water, CO₂ transport and storage)
    - Uncertain demand for high-cost CO₂ removals
    - Public acceptance and permitting

---

**4. Three to Five Plausible Futures**

**A. Breakthrough Scenario (Low Probability)**
- Major technological advances (e.g., new sorbents, energy integration) drive costs below $100/tCO₂ by 2030.
- Aggressive policy (global carbon price, mandates) creates reliable demand.
- DAC scales to >1 Gt/year by 2035, achieving cost parity with fossil fuels for carbon removal.

**B. Incremental Progress (Most Likely)**
- Costs fall to $150–$300/tCO₂ by 2035 due to steady innovation and scale.
- DAC deployment reaches 50–200 Mt/year by 2035.
- Fossil fuels remain cheaper, but DAC fills a niche for hard-to-abate emissions and premium offset markets.

**C. Stagnation**
- Costs remain high ($400+/tCO₂), supply chain and policy hurdles persist.
- DAC deployment plateaus below 10 Mt/year.
- Negative emissions targets shift to alternative solutions.

**D. Policy-Led Expansion**
- Massive government intervention (mandates, contracts-for-difference, direct procurement) drives up deployment despite high costs.
- DAC scales to 0.5–1 Gt/year by 2035, but at significant public expense.
- Cost parity with fossil fuels is not achieved, but negative emissions goals are prioritized.

---

**5. Key Assumptions**

- **Learning Rates:** Assumes DAC can achieve 10–20% cost reductions per doubling of capacity, similar to solar PV or batteries.
- **Policy Stability:** Assumes continued or increased policy support (subsidies, tax credits, procurement).
- **Market Formation:** Assumes a robust market for high-quality carbon removals emerges.
- **Technological Breakthroughs:** Assumes no unforeseen breakthroughs or, conversely, no major setbacks.
- **Competing Solutions:** Assumes no disruptive advances in alternative negative emissions (e.g., enhanced weathering, ocean-based CDR).

---

**6. Predictions with Time Horizons**

- **By 2025:** DAC costs remain >$400/tCO₂; global capacity <0.1 Mt/year. Early demonstration projects and government pilots dominate.
- **By 2030:** Costs fall to $200–$400/tCO₂ for leading projects; capacity reaches 5–20 Mt/year. Early commercial markets (aviation, voluntary offsets) grow.
- **By 2035:** Costs reach $150–$300/tCO₂ for best-in-class projects. Global capacity could reach 50–200 Mt/year under optimistic scenarios. Achieving 1 Gt/year is unlikely without massive policy intervention. Cost parity with fossil fuels remains out of reach unless fossil fuel prices rise sharply or DAC costs fall unexpectedly.

---

**7. Signals That Would Change the Forecast**

- **Rapid Cost Declines:** Announcements of DAC projects operating below $100/tCO₂ at scale.
- **Major Policy Shifts:** Introduction of global carbon pricing or mandatory CDR procurement at gigaton scale.
- **Breakthrough Technologies:** Demonstrated, scalable new DAC chemistries or processes with step-change efficiency.
- **Supply Chain Mobilization:** Major investments in sorbent manufacturing, renewable energy integration, and CO₂ storage infrastructure.
- **Corporate Demand:** Long-term, billion-dollar offtake agreements from major emitters.

---

**8. What a Builder, Policymaker, Investor, and Citizen Should Watch**

- **Builder:** Track advances in sorbent materials, modular plant designs, and integration with low-carbon energy sources.
- **Policymaker:** Monitor cost curves, deployment rates, and effectiveness of subsidies/tax credits. Watch for international policy coordination and public acceptance.
- **Investor:** Look for signs of market formation (long-term contracts, credible buyers), technology differentiation, and supply chain readiness.
- **Citizen:** Follow public debates on negative emissions, land/water use impacts, and the transparency of carbon removal claims.

---

**9. Best Next Questions**

- What are the most promising pathways for radical DAC cost reduction?
- How can policy frameworks best support early deployment and learning-by-doing?
- What are the environmental and social impacts of DAC at scale?
- How does DAC compare to other negative emissions technologies in terms of scalability, permanence, and cost?
- What mechanisms can ensure the integrity and additionality of DAC-based CO₂ removals?
